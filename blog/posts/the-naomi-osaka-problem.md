Imagine you're Naomi Osaka, playing against one of the greatest female tennis players on earth. That's right, you're playing against THE Serena Williams. She has been your childhood idol, and it feels surreal to even share a court with her. But this is tennis, and you've got to play no matter who is on the other side. You've got to try to beat even your childhood idol. This is competitive battle; in wars, people go as far as killing one another. Would it be selfish of you to try to beat her? Would others applaud your win? Would it be your fault if you actually beat her?

## The story

In case you're wondering, I'm not coming up with a hypothetical scenario but rather trying to picture what actually happened during the 2018 US Open final between 20-year-old Naomi Osaka and 37-year-old Serena Williams. Naomi was the underdog going into this match, but she played spectacularly that day. She was beating Serena fair and square. During the game, Serena Williams got a warning for receiving coaching tips from her coach, who was sitting among the crowd. This is illegal and, as a result, Serena got a warning from the match umpire (don't ask me how I know this word). She was so frustrated during the game that she smashed her tennis racket and got a penalty for her behavior. Honestly, I think this was unprofessional from Serena. Maybe it was hard for her to accept defeat by a younger generation, but she was doing it out of frustration, and I would say it was childish.

In the end, Naomi did beat Serena in that match and she was the Grand Slam winner. Unfortunately, during the award ceremony the crowd was booing her. As a result, she felt guilty and started crying. She even went on to apologize for all of this. The thing is, none of this is her fault, but she was feeling guilty about it. But why should anyone feel guilty about winning? I can understand it's hard to actually go and try to beat your childhood idol. But you shouldn't feel guilty about it either. After all, it's a match and there must be a winner.

## The Naomi Problem

As I have mentioned, Naomi shouldn't have felt bad about her win. Of course, out of respect for her idol she shouldn't have been jumping in the air out of happiness. But I have seen Naomi having similar problems where she is trying to be a highly moral person and as a result she ends up feeling bad about it. I think it negatively affects her. I believe trying to be morally right all the time in this world is a heavy burden. Instead, she should have wanted the win against Serena even if it makes her behave like a child. Yes, that's right: how your opponent behaves is not your problem. You can't join a match and expect the other party to feel good. Wanting to win doesn't make you less of a moral person. It just means you prioritize yourself. Don't feel guilty for winning.

But much respect goes to Serena for telling the crowd to stop booing and congratulating her opponent who beat her fair and square.
